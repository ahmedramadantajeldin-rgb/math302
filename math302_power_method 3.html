§ 01

Fundamental Concepts

Eigenvalue Problem — Definition

فكرة الـ Eigenvalue بتقول: لو عندك مصفوفة \( A \)، فيه متجهات خاصة اسمها eigenvectors — لما بنضرب فيها المصفوفة، ما بتتغيرش اتجاهها، بس بتتضاعف بعامل عددي اسمه eigenvalue.

يعني المصفوفة بتعمل زي كإنها بس بتمط أو بتعكس المتجه ده، مش بتغير اتجاهه.

\[ A\,\vec{x} = \lambda\,\vec{x} \]

Where \( \vec{x} \neq \vec{0} \) is the eigenvector (direction that remains unchanged under \( A \)) and \( \lambda \) is the corresponding eigenvalue (the scalar factor).

Theoretical Derivation

عشان نوصل لطريقة نحسب بيها الـ eigenvalue، هننقل كل حاجة لجنب واحد:

\[ A\vec{x} = \lambda\vec{x} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad A\vec{x} - \lambda\vec{x} = \vec{0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (A - \lambda I)\,\vec{x} = \vec{0} \]

For a non-trivial solution (\( \vec{x} \neq \vec{0} \)) to exist, the matrix \( (A - \lambda I) \) must be singular:

\[ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \]

This is the characteristic equation. Solving it analytically gives eigenvalues exactly, but for large matrices the Power Method provides an iterative numerical approximation to the dominant eigenvalue (the one with the largest absolute value).

§ 02

Power Method — Algorithm

Core Idea & Intuition

Power Method هي أبسط وأسرع طريقة عددية للإيجاد الـ dominant eigenvalue (القيمة الذاتية الأكبر مطلقاً) لمصفوفة.

الفكرة: بنبدأ بمتجه ابتدائي عشوائي \( X^{(0)} \)، وبنضرب فيه المصفوفة \( A \) مرة وراء مرة. مع تكرار الضرب، المتجه بيتجه ناحية الـ eigenvector المقابل للـ eigenvalue الأكبر. والنسبة بين عناصر الناتج الجديد للقديم بتقترب من الـ eigenvalue نفسه.

هنعرف إننا وصلنا للتقارب Convergence لما نسب عناصر المتجه الجديد على القديم تبقى متساوية (أو قريبة من بعض).

Iterative Formula
\[ X^{(n+1)} = A\,X^{(n)} \]

Starting from an initial guess \( X^{(0)} \), each iteration multiplies by \( A \) to produce the next approximation. The eigenvalue is estimated by the element-wise ratio:

\[ \lambda \approx \frac{X^{(n+1)}_i}{X^{(n)}_i} \quad\text{for each component } i \]

When all ratios converge to the same value → that value is the dominant eigenvalue \( \lambda \).

Convergence Criterion

Not Converged: Ratios \( X^{(n+1)}_i / X^{(n)}_i \) differ significantly between components.

Converged: All ratios (for nonzero components) approach the same value \( \lambda \). The iteration vector \( X^{(n)} \) is the approximate eigenvector.

Solution Procedure

اتبع الخطوات دي بالترتيب في كل مثال:

1
Start with initial vector \( X^{(0)} \) — typically \( (1, 0, 0, \ldots)^T \) unless otherwise specified. المتجه الابتدائي بنختاره بسيط. المهم إنه مش الصفر.
2
Compute \( X^{(n+1)} = A \cdot X^{(n)} \) — a standard matrix-vector multiplication. كل عنصر في المتجه الجديد = الصف المقابل من \( A \) مضروب نقطياً في المتجه القديم.
3
Compute ratios: divide each component of \( X^{(n+1)} \) by the corresponding component of \( X^{(n)} \). تجاهل المكونات اللي قيمتها صفر في القسمة — مش معرّفة.
4
Check convergence: if ratios are equal (or close) → stopped; that ratio is \( \lambda \) and \( X^{(n+1)} \) is the eigenvector. Otherwise → repeat from step 2.
§ 03

Example 1

01
Power Method · 3 Iterations · 3×3 Matrix
Obtain an approximate eigenpair \( (\lambda,\, X) \) for the matrix \( A \) using initial vector \( X^{(0)} = (1,\,0,\,0)^T \). Perform 3 iterations.
Given Data
\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 10 & -4 & -8 \\ -4 & 4 & 4 \\ -8 & 4 & 10 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad X^{(0)} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
Iter 1 \( X^{(1)} = A\,X^{(0)} \)
\[ X^{(1)} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 & -4 & -8 \\ -4 & 4 & 4 \\ -8 & 4 & 10 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} (10)(1)+(-4)(0)+(-8)(0) \\ (-4)(1)+(4)(0)+(4)(0) \\ (-8)(1)+(4)(0)+(10)(0) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ -4 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio check — \( X^{(1)} / X^{(0)} \): division by zero in components 2 and 3 → Not converged

Iter 2 \( X^{(2)} = A\,X^{(1)} \)
\[ X^{(2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 & -4 & -8 \\ -4 & 4 & 4 \\ -8 & 4 & 10 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ -4 \\ -8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 100+16+64 \\ -40-16-32 \\ -80-16-80 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 180 \\ -88 \\ -176 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio check — \( X^{(2)}_i \,/\, X^{(1)}_i \):

Component \( X^{(1)}_i \) \( X^{(2)}_i \) Ratio \( X^{(2)}/X^{(1)} \) Status
1st 10 180 18 Not converged
18 ≠ 22
2nd -4 -88 22
3rd -8 -176 22
Iter 3 \( X^{(3)} = A\,X^{(2)} \)
\[ X^{(3)} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 & -4 & -8 \\ -4 & 4 & 4 \\ -8 & 4 & 10 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 180 \\ -88 \\ -176 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1800+352+1408 \\ -720-352-704 \\ -1440-352-1760 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3560 \\ -1776 \\ -3552 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio check — \( X^{(3)}_i \,/\, X^{(2)}_i \):

Component \( X^{(2)}_i \) \( X^{(3)}_i \) Ratio \( X^{(3)}/X^{(2)} \) Status
1st 180 3560 ≈ 19.78 Converging
19.78 ≈ 20.18
2nd -88 -1776 ≈ 20.18
3rd -176 -3552 ≈ 20.18
Two out of three ratios are identical at 20.18, and the first is approaching that value. The iteration is converging to the dominant eigenvalue.
✓
Dominant Eigenvalue (after 3 iterations)
\( \lambda \approx 20.18 \)
Eigenvector: \( X^{(3)} = (3560,\; -1776,\; -3552)^T \)

Exercise 1

03
Power Method · Block-Diagonal Structure · 4 Iterations
Calculate the dominant eigenvalue and eigenvector for matrix \( A \) using \( X^{(0)} = (1,\,0,\,0)^T \).
Given Data
\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 & 0 \\ 8 & -6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad X^{(0)} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
Note on Matrix Structure: This matrix has a block-diagonal structure — the top-left 2×2 block \( \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 8 & -6 \end{pmatrix} \) is decoupled from the bottom-right scalar \( 5 \). Eigenvalues of the 2×2 block: \( (6-\lambda)(-6-\lambda) - 64 = \lambda^2 - 100 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda = \pm 10 \). All eigenvalues: \( \{+10,\,-10,\,5\} \). Since \( |{+10}| = |{-10}| \), the two dominant eigenvalues have equal magnitude, which causes the standard Power Method to oscillate rather than converge to a single ratio. The two-step ratio analysis (shown below) resolves this.
Iter 1 \( X^{(1)} = A\,X^{(0)} \)
\[ X^{(1)} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 & 0 \\ 8 & -6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 8 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio \( X^{(1)}/X^{(0)} \): \( 6/1=6,\quad 8/0=\infty \) → Not converged

Iter 2 \( X^{(2)} = A\,X^{(1)} \)
\[ X^{(2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 & 0 \\ 8 & -6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 8 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 36+64 \\ 48-48 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 100 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio: \( 100/6 \approx 16.67,\quad 0/8 = 0 \) → Not converged — oscillating

Iter 3 \( X^{(3)} = A\,X^{(2)} \)
\[ X^{(3)} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 & 0 \\ 8 & -6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 100 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 600 \\ 800 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio: \( 600/100=6,\quad 800/0=\infty \) → Still oscillating

Iter 4 \( X^{(4)} = A\,X^{(3)} \)
\[ X^{(4)} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 & 0 \\ 8 & -6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 600 \\ 800 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3600+6400 \\ 4800-4800 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10000 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio: \( 10000/600 \approx 16.67,\quad 0/800=0 \) → Still oscillating

Two-Step Ratio Analysis

لما الـ ratios تتأرجح بين قيمتين بدل ما تتقارب، ده معناه إن فيه eigenvalues متساوية في المطلق بس مختلفة في الإشارة (زي \(+10\) و \(-10\)).

الحل: بدل ما نقسم خطوة على الخطوة السابقة، نقسم على خطوتين قبلها — ده بيعطينا \( \lambda^2 \) وبالتالي \( |\lambda| \).

Two-step ratio:

\[ \frac{X^{(4)}_1}{X^{(2)}_1} = \frac{10000}{100} = 100 = \lambda^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad |\lambda| = \sqrt{100} = 10 \] \[ \frac{X^{(3)}_1}{X^{(1)}_1} = \frac{600}{6} = 100 = \lambda^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad |\lambda| = 10 \quad \checkmark \]

The two dominant eigenvalues are \( \lambda = +10 \) and \( \lambda = -10 \). The corresponding eigenvectors (solved from \( (A-\lambda I)\vec{x}=0 \)) are:

\[ \lambda = +10 \;\Rightarrow\; v_1 = (2,\,1,\,0)^T \qquad\quad \lambda = -10 \;\Rightarrow\; v_2 = (1,\,-2,\,0)^T \]
✓
Dominant Eigenvalue (by two-step analysis)
\( |\lambda| = 10 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda = +10 \;\text{and}\; \lambda = -10 \)
Eigenvector for \( \lambda=+10 \): \(\; (2,\;1,\;0)^T \)\quad·\quad Eigenvector for \( \lambda=-10 \): \(\; (1,\;-2,\;0)^T \)

04
Power Method · Symmetric Matrix · 4 Iterations
Calculate the dominant eigenvalue and eigenvector for matrix \( A \) using \( X^{(0)} = (1,\,0,\,0)^T \).
Given Data
\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 5 & -6 \\ -1 & -6 & 8 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad X^{(0)} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
Iter 1 \( X^{(1)} = A\,X^{(0)} \)
\[ X^{(1)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 5 & -6 \\ -1 & -6 & 8 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio: \( 0/1=0,\quad 2/0=\infty \) → Not converged

Iter 2 \( X^{(2)} = A\,X^{(1)} \)
\[ X^{(2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 5 & -6 \\ -1 & -6 & 8 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0+4+1 \\ 0+10+6 \\ 0-12-8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 16 \\ -20 \end{pmatrix} \]

Ratio \( X^{(2)}/X^{(1)} \): \( 5/0=\infty,\quad 16/2=8,\quad -20/-1=20 \) → Not converged

Iter 3 \( X^{(3)} = A\,X^{(2)} \)
\[ X^{(3)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 5 & -6 \\ -1 & -6 & 8 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 16 \\ -20 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0+32+20 \\ 10+80+120 \\ -5-96-160 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 52 \\ 210 \\ -261 \end{pmatrix} \]
Component \( X^{(2)}_i \) \( X^{(3)}_i \) Ratio Status

→ ≈ 13
2nd16210 13.125
3rd-20-261 13.05
Iter 4 \( X^{(4)} = A\,X^{(3)} \)
\[ X^{(4)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 5 & -6 \\ -1 & -6 & 8 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 52 \\ 210 \\ -261 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0+420+261 \\ 104+1050+1566 \\ -52-1260-2088 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 681 \\ 2720 \\ -3400 \end{pmatrix} \]

All three ratios are converging to the same value \( \approx 13 \). The exact dominant eigenvalue (from the characteristic polynomial \( \lambda^3 - 13\lambda^2 - \lambda + 13 = (\lambda^2-1)(\lambda-13) = 0 \)) is \( \lambda = 13 \), confirming our iteration.
✓
Dominant Eigenvalue (after 4 iterations)
\( \lambda = 13 \)
Eigenvector: \( X^{(4)} = (681,\; 2720,\; -3400)^T \)
